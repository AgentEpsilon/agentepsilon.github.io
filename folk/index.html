In 1928, Robert W. Gordon came to the Library of Congress as head of the newly-created Archive of American Folk-Song. He collected a wide range of songs from a variety of informants, making sound recordings of the traditional singers he found in order to preserve the melodies of the songs.

Amazing Grace

John A. Lomax, a Texas folklorist, feared that the radio and gramaphone would discourage people from making their own music. During the 1930's and 40's, he traveled throughout the South with his son, Alan, and a recording machine. Together they visited farms, ranches, schoolyards, churches, night clubs, prisons... anywhere they could find music to record.

Gypsy Davy

Operating with similar motives to other ethnographers, Frances Densmore, Helen Roberts, Willard Rhodes, and others documented Native American Music. They feared that American Indians displaced from their lands were at risk of losing their culture. Recording the music was especially important for this undertaking, as Indian song texts are often composed purely of nonlexical syllables, such as hey or na.
